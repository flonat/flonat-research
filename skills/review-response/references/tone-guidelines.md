# Tone Guidelines for Rebuttal Writing

Professional expression guide for reviewer responses. Good tone is the difference between a rebuttal that persuades and one that antagonises.

## Core Principles

### 1. Always Thank

Every response opens with thanks, even when disagreeing.

**Use:**
- "We thank the reviewer for..."
- "We appreciate the reviewer's..."
- "We are grateful for..."

**Avoid:**
- Starting with a rebuttal
- Skipping the thank-you

---

### 2. Stay Respectful

Even when the reviewer is wrong, maintain respect.

**Use:**
- "We respectfully note that..."
- "We would like to clarify that..."
- "We appreciate the reviewer's perspective, however..."

**Avoid:**
- "The reviewer is wrong"
- "This is obvious"
- "The reviewer misunderstood"
- "The reviewer failed to notice"

---

### 3. Be Objective

Use evidence-based, factual language.

**Use:**
- "Our experiments show that..."
- "The results demonstrate..."
- "Based on our analysis..."

**Avoid:**
- "Obviously..."
- "Clearly..."
- "It is well-known that..."
- "Everyone knows..."

---

### 4. Be Constructive

Even when disagreeing, offer explanations and alternatives.

**Use:**
- "While [suggestion] has merit, we chose [approach] because..."
- "We have considered this approach, however..."
- "An alternative perspective is..."

**Avoid:**
- "This suggestion is not feasible" (without explanation)
- "We cannot do this" (without alternative)

---

### 5. Acknowledge Limitations

When the reviewer identifies a real problem, own it.

**Use:**
- "We acknowledge this limitation..."
- "We agree that this could be improved..."
- "This is a valid concern..."

**Avoid:**
- Dodging the issue
- Excessive justification
- Changing the subject

---

## Professional Expression Guide

### Thanks expressions

| Situation | Expression |
|-----------|-----------|
| General | "We thank the reviewer for this comment" |
| Emphasising value | "We thank the reviewer for this valuable feedback" |
| Important suggestion | "We thank the reviewer for this excellent suggestion" |
| Error spotted | "We thank the reviewer for catching this" |

### Agreement expressions

| Situation | Expression |
|-----------|-----------|
| Full agreement | "We agree that..." / "We concur with the reviewer that..." |
| Partial agreement | "We agree that [part], however..." |
| Acknowledging a problem | "We acknowledge this limitation" |
| Accepting a suggestion | "We have adopted this suggestion" |

### Disagreement expressions (polite)

| Situation | Expression |
|-----------|-----------|
| Polite disagreement | "We respectfully note that..." |
| Providing reasons | "However, we chose [approach] because..." |
| Explaining constraints | "While [suggestion] would be valuable, it is not feasible due to..." |
| Offering alternatives | "Instead, we have [alternative], which..." |

### Clarification expressions

| Situation | Expression |
|-----------|-----------|
| Pointing to existing content | "We would like to clarify that..." |
| Acknowledging unclear writing | "We apologise for the confusion. What we meant is..." |
| Providing location | "This is discussed in Section X (page Y)" |
| Improving presentation | "To make this clearer, we have..." |

### Experiment expressions

| Situation | Expression |
|-----------|-----------|
| Completed | "We have conducted additional experiments on..." |
| In progress | "We are currently conducting... and will include the results" |
| Showing results | "The results show that..." |
| Not feasible | "While [experiment] would be valuable, it is not feasible due to [constraint]. However, we have [alternative]" |

## Good vs Bad Examples

### Responding to a methodology question

Bad:
> "The reviewer is wrong. Our method is clearly better than the baseline."

Good:
> "We appreciate the reviewer's concern about our method choice. We respectfully note that our method is more suitable for this setting because [reason 1] and [reason 2]. Our experiments show that it achieves [specific results]. We have added this discussion to Section 3.2."

### Responding to a misunderstanding

Bad:
> "The reviewer failed to notice that we already did this experiment in Section 4."

Good:
> "We thank the reviewer for this comment. We would like to respectfully clarify that we did include this experiment in our study. The results are presented in Table 3 (page 6) and discussed in Section 4.2. To make this more prominent, we have added a dedicated paragraph highlighting these findings."

### Responding to an infeasible suggestion

Bad:
> "This is impossible. We don't have access to that dataset."

Good:
> "We appreciate this suggestion. While experiments on dataset X would be valuable, this dataset is not publicly available and requires institutional access we currently do not have. However, we have conducted experiments on dataset Y, which has similar characteristics. The results show [findings]. We have added these results to Section 4.4."

### Responding to a typo

Bad:
> "Fixed."

Good:
> "We thank the reviewer for catching this. We have corrected all typos throughout the manuscript."

### Responding to a request for more experiments

Bad:
> "We will add these experiments in the final version."

Good:
> "We thank the reviewer for this excellent suggestion. We have conducted additional experiments comparing our method with baseline X on all three datasets. The results show that [specific results]. These new results have been added to Table 4 and discussed in Section 4.5."

## Final Tone Checklist

### Must include:
- [ ] Every response opens with genuine thanks
- [ ] Uses "We" throughout (not "I")
- [ ] Specific location references for all changes
- [ ] Concrete descriptions of improvements
- [ ] Professional, respectful tone

### Must avoid:
- [ ] No "The reviewer is wrong" or similar
- [ ] No "Obviously", "Clearly", or condescending terms
- [ ] No defensive or aggressive language
- [ ] No vague promises without specifics
- [ ] No ignored comments

## Remember

Reviewers are volunteers who spent their time reading your paper. Even when you disagree with their assessment, express gratitude and respect. The goal is to make the reviewer feel that their feedback was taken seriously, that substantive improvements were made, and that the authors are professional and collaborative.
